# Deck Design Patterns

These patterns describe presentation choices that TasteCraft skills should be able to reason about. They are intentionally tool-agnostic.

## One Page, One Job

Every slide should have a clear objective before layout starts. Good objectives sound like:

- "Show why retention improved despite lower acquisition."
- "Explain the three decision gates for rollout."
- "Make the board approve the recovery plan."

Weak objectives sound like:

- "Talk about data."
- "Include background."
- "Add visuals."

## Density Matching

Use density based on audience behavior:

- Speaker-led: low text, strong hierarchy, one memorable point.
- Balanced: enough detail for live discussion and later review.
- Reading-first: self-contained page with clear evidence.
- Appendix-heavy: dense reference material with strong labels and source notes.

## Visual Role Before Visual Style

Choose the role before choosing the visual:

- Evidence: chart, table, or annotated metric.
- Structure: framework, sequence, or architecture map.
- Emotion: carefully controlled hero image or metaphor.
- Navigation: chapter divider, agenda, or progress marker.
- Action: decision table, checklist, or owner timeline.

## Bilingual Labeling

For bilingual-ish decks, keep the primary message in one language and use the second language for short labels, section anchors, or domain terms. Do not duplicate every sentence unless the audience requires full bilingual reading.

## Logo Restraint

Logo placement should follow purpose:

- Cover-only: best for public-facing decks.
- Footer-subtle: acceptable for formal internal decks.
- Section-only: useful when chapters need brand rhythm.
- None: safest for public synthetic examples.
